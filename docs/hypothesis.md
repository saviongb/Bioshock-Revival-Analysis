Bioshock (Remastered):



What it does well:

Being released in 2007, the original BioShock was truly bold and innovative in numerous regards. Stylistically, the art deco style works perfectly in the dystopia of rapture. Mechanically, the combat is truly engaging with both a left hand plasmid ability and right hand weapon, allowing for creativity that immerses you within the level design (water, barrels, turrets). Which weapon would work best with this plasmid? Which plasmid would work best for this area? Bioshock encourages creativity and rewards players for it. Story progression is managed well, and the game has a theatrical way of introducing the player to new concepts, enemies and mechanics. The game is generally dark, but still manages to feel colorful and visually stunning. The level design feels far from tedious, and creative cutscenes built into the world create a feeling that we are walking through a theatre act. The looting system feels meaningful and pairs well with a stimulating hacking process.



Where it struggles:

It’s hard to criticize a game that did so much with what it had to work with. This game is heavily built on narrative, and it is hard to stray from that, so players who are not able to express their creativity through the combat system may feel a bit restricted in terms of exploration or imagination. There is a bit of a rinse and repeat feel with the lack of enemy models.



Final thoughts:

Recurring horror and theatre elements give an impression that we are falling into a trap laid out for us, despite having all the tools we need to handle it. This feel ties perfectly into the philosophy of BioShock 1, the matter of free will and a phrase that had been repeated throughout the game.

Bioshock 1’s positive steam reviews will ride on a strong emphasis for narrative and atmosphere, showcasing an appreciation for rich storytelling. Negative steam reviews will highlight a sense of repetition and tediousness in combat.









Bioshock 2 (Remastered):



What it does well: 

Released only a few years later, Bioshock 2 is a phenomenal sequel to its predecessor. A completely refined combat system is at the forefront of what makes Bioshock 2 so fun to play. A combat system that allows for the simultaneous use of both plasmids and weapons allowed for the developers to offer more enemy engagements. The addition of hack darts (and improved hacking system), the drill, and your new responsibility of defending saving/defending little sisters takes the mechanics of BioShock to another level. The narrative and storytelling no longer feel at the forefront of the game's identity, and while this isn't a knock against either game, it is a refreshing change of pace. Dialogue from radio broadcasters and audio diaries still guides the narrative, and it does so in a way that captures the same feel as the first.



Where it struggles: 

The remastered version crashes repeatedly through playthroughs, although frequent saves makes this manageable. The storytelling feels very familiar, I did not see an issue with this as I was enamored by the combat, although players who rely on a rich narrative may find it less enticing than the first.



Finals thoughts:

Leans slightly away from a sense of horror and relies more on an emotional connection to your “little sister”, this is something that I bought into. We feel less of a sense of cautiously navigating through an ominous city of exploitation and more that we are a force to be reckoned with. The dialogue speaks to us in a way that recognizes our power yet tries to stop it.

Bioshock 2’s positive steam reviews will emphasize improved combat mechanics (dual wield, unique weapons) and engagements while negative steam reviews will highlight less impactful storytelling elements and technical stability (crashes)







Bioshock Infinite:



What it does well:

Truly outstanding introduction sequence, I was in awe at the creativity and parallels to various political and religious ideology. If Bioshock 1 and 2 were both underwater, the only logical option would be to blast us into the sky, right? New mechanics are introduced in quantum tears, skylines and reworked abilities with vigor. For someone that grew such a strong connection to the first 2 BioShock's and their “soul”, it was easy to see where infinite innovated. It seems as though the developers understood their player base's appreciation for a rich story and did not hold back. One of my favorite aspects and something that could go un-noticed is the addition of NPC’s to guide us through the game's narrative. Primarily handled by radio broadcasts and audio diaries in the first 2, we now have a more interactive method for story progression. I think for players who loved BioShock 1 for its storytelling would find infinite to be an outstanding and mind altering progression. It is not often you get such a captivating and thought provoking story and encompasses matters of free will, greed, racism, politics and religion in a way that allows you to navigate through it.



Where it struggled:

Perhaps I am too biased to the original format of BioShock games, but I found certain aspects to be far less inspiring than the first 2 games. Beginning with the combat, there was a sense of uniqueness and charm about the weapons in the first 2 BioShock games, this feeling that it could only exist in this context (rivet gun, drill), this felt missing in infinite. As for the vigor's, their use case was still warranted, although it felt less immersive with respect to the level design. Understandably, the focus on tears took some of this use case away from vigor, although it felt far less satisfying to open a tear than to electrify a puddle of water in Bioshock 2. Essentially, there wasn't enough opportunity for most of the vigor's to be useful. Infinite was brighter, although the world design grew stale for me as the game progressed, instead of walking through an underwater dystopian city, we were walking through something that resembled reality. As for narrative, Booker speaks to characters throughout the game through dialogue that we can hear, this reduced immersion for me and made it feel more like I was clicking buttons to watch a film. In the first 2 games, it felt as though we were in control of how we responded and felt to what occurred, although in infinite, Booker decides for us. This also mitigated the connection I developed to characters like Elizabeth, she's talking to Booker, not the player. 



Final notes:

While Bioshock Infinite was not my favorite of the franchise, it did encapsulate what I truly love about video games the most, their ability to express art and process reality.
